## 6.5 Years, 4,612 Contributions, One Email

There is a strange kind of grief that comes with a layoff. Not the sharp, sudden kind — but the slow, hollow kind. The kind where you sit at your desk on a Tuesday afternoon and realise that the thing you gave six and a half years of your life to has decided it no longer needs you.

I joined Block as a software engineer. I left — or rather, was let go — as a Staff Software Engineer. In between, there were thousands of commits, countless code reviews, late nights debugging production incidents, and the quiet satisfaction of watching systems I built serve millions of people.

---

## The Commit Graph Tells a Story

I look at my GitHub contribution graphs and each green square is a small piece of my life. Not just code — but mornings spent thinking about architecture in the shower, weekends where "one quick fix" turned into six hours, and the pride of shipping something that actually mattered.

2022 — 1,201 contributions. The year I was promoted to Staff. Bigger scope, bigger responsibilities, bigger expectations. The graph is dense and green. I was building.

![GitHub contributions in 2022 — 1,201 contributions](/life-after-6pm/year-2022.png)

2023 — 1,044 contributions. Still consistent. Still shipping. The slight dip didn't mean I was slowing down — it meant the work had shifted. More design docs, more mentoring, more of the invisible work that Staff engineers do. The kind of work that doesn't show up in a commit count.

![GitHub contributions in 2023 — 1,044 contributions](/life-after-6pm/year-2023.png)

2024 — 1,154 contributions. The numbers went back up. Something changed in how I worked. AI-assisted development had quietly become part of my workflow — Copilot filling in boilerplate, Claude helping me think through edge cases, automated tools catching what I used to catch by hand. The nature of what a "commit" meant was shifting beneath my feet.

![GitHub contributions in 2024 — 1,154 contributions](/life-after-6pm/year-2024.png)

And now — 1,213 contributions in the last year. The graph doesn't know that somewhere in those green squares, a decision was made in a boardroom I'll never see, by people who don't know my name, that my position was no longer needed.

![GitHub contributions in the last year — 1,213 contributions](/life-after-6pm/now.png)

## A Word to Leaders

An engineer is a crew member on your ship. When you abandon ship — when you let people go not because they failed but because the spreadsheet demanded it — you lose something that doesn't show up on a balance sheet. You lose the trust of every person who once believed in you. Every engineer who stayed late because they believed the mission mattered. Every Staff engineer who turned down offers from elsewhere because they thought loyalty was a two-way street.

It turns out loyalty is a one-way street with a dead end.

---

## What AI Changed — and What It Didn't

Looking at those contribution graphs, I can see the inflection point where AI started changing my work. The commits got faster. The boilerplate disappeared. The code reviews got sharper because I had an AI pair programmer helping me spot patterns I might have missed.

But here's the thing nobody talks about: AI didn't replace the hard parts. It didn't design the system architecture. It didn't navigate the politics of a cross-team migration. It didn't sit in the incident room at 2am making decisions under pressure. It didn't mentor the junior engineer who was struggling with imposter syndrome.

AI made the mechanical parts faster. The thinking parts — the parts that actually matter — those are still human. Still mine.

## Life After 6PM

For six and a half years, "after 6PM" meant checking Slack one more time. Reviewing one more PR. Thinking about tomorrow's standup. The boundary between work and life had dissolved so completely that I forgot it was supposed to exist.

Now, after 6PM is just... mine. And I'm building this — a journal app, ironically using the same AI tools that changed how I worked at Block. This entire application was scaffolded with Claude. The rich text editor you're reading this in was configured by an AI assistant. The deployment pipeline was written in minutes, not hours.

I was let go like a piece of paper — six and a half years of devotion folded up and filed away. But paper can be unfolded. Paper can be written on again.

The future of software engineering is not fewer engineers. It's engineers who can move at the speed of thought, augmented by AI, building things that used to take teams of ten in an afternoon by themselves. And I intend to be one of them.

This is my first entry. After 6PM, the code is mine again.
